VICTORIAN FAIRY PAINTING INSPIRED EXHIBITION

Where folklore meets fear, and fear becomes culture.

This exhibit explores how people across cultures have turned uncertainty into stories, rituals, and warning signs. What looks irrational at first often reveals a deeply human need for meaning, control, and protection.

COLLECTION OF MISFORTUNE

Gallery of Superstitions

13

The Number 13

History: Fear of 13, or triskaidekaphobia, has often been linked to religious and mythological traditions in which 13 signals disorder or betrayal.

Psychology: It reflects pattern-seeking behavior and the tendency to assign meaning to coincidence.

🐈

Black Cats

History: In medieval Europe, black cats became tied to witchcraft and dark omens.

Psychology: This shows how social storytelling can shape fear and reinforce irrational associations.

✂

Cutting Nails at Night

History: In some Asian traditions, cutting nails after dark was seen as inviting danger or misfortune, likely rooted in older safety concerns.

Psychology: Practical warnings can become symbolic beliefs when repeated across generations.

🪞

Breaking a Mirror

History: Ancient beliefs treated mirrors as spiritually significant, so breaking one was thought to damage the self and invite bad luck.

Psychology: This reflects how humans project identity and emotional meaning onto objects.

CURATORIAL NOTES

Process and Interpretation

This digital museum was designed as a small, focused exhibition using a Victorian fairy painting aesthetic: dark jewel tones, soft glowing contrast, ornate framing, and an atmosphere of mystery.

The site uses the Magician brand archetype to create a sense of hidden meaning and transformation. It also uses Authority as a persuasive principle by presenting each superstition through curated labels, structured interpretation, and explanatory notes.
