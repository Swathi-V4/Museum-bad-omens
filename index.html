Why Is the Number 13 Feared?

Western tradition

Hover to reveal

The Number 13

History: Fear of 13, known as triskaidekaphobia, is often linked to Western religious and mythological traditions in which the number signals disorder, imbalance, or betrayal.

Psychology: This reflects pattern-seeking behavior and the tendency to remember negative events more strongly when they confirm an existing fear.

Why It Persists: The number is still avoided in buildings, hotels, and travel settings, which helps the belief feel culturally reinforced and real.

Why Does a Black Cat Feel Like a Warning?

European folklore

Hover to reveal

Black Cats

History: In parts of medieval Europe, black cats became associated with witchcraft, night, and dark omens, turning an ordinary animal into a symbol of danger.

Psychology: This shows how social storytelling can attach fear to visual symbols and create strong emotional associations that outlast their original context.

Why It Persists: Because black cats are visually striking and repeatedly used in folklore and media, the superstition stays recognizable across generations.

What Happens If You Cut Your Nails at Night?

Asian household belief

Hover to reveal

Cutting Nails at Night

History: In some Asian cultures, cutting nails after dark was believed to invite bad luck or danger, possibly because poor lighting once made the act risky or impractical.

Psychology: Practical warnings can slowly become symbolic beliefs when they are repeated through family instruction and tied to imagined consequences.

Why It Persists: The superstition survives because it blends daily routine with tradition, making it easy to pass down as both advice and cautionary belief.

Why Does Breaking a Mirror Mean Bad Luck?

Ancient omen

Hover to reveal

Breaking a Mirror

History: Ancient cultures often viewed mirrors as spiritually significant objects connected to identity and the soul, so breaking one was thought to damage more than glass.

Psychology: Humans project emotional meaning onto personal objects, especially ones tied to reflection and self-image, which makes the superstition feel intimate and unsettling.

Why It Persists: A shattered mirror is dramatic, memorable, and visually symbolic, making the story easy to repeat and hard to forget.

Why Do We Throw Salt Over Our Shoulder?

European custom

Hover to reveal

Spilling Salt

History: Salt was once expensive and symbolically valuable, so spilling it could represent waste, misfortune, or the disruption of order.

Psychology: People often give extra meaning to accidents when the object involved is already culturally marked as important.

Why It Persists: The ritual of throwing salt over the shoulder makes the superstition memorable because it turns a mistake into a dramatic act of correction.

Why Is Walking Under a Ladder Considered Dangerous?

Western superstition

Hover to reveal

Walking Under a Ladder

History: Ladders were once associated with sacred triangular forms and with spaces of labor, danger, and punishment, making passage beneath them feel risky or disrespectful.

Psychology: This superstition may survive because it combines symbolic danger with a practical truth: walking under a ladder can genuinely be unsafe.

Why It Persists: Beliefs endure more easily when they are reinforced by both cultural storytelling and common-sense caution.

Opening an Umbrella Indoors

History: This belief may have developed from earlier concerns about damaging indoor spaces or accidentally hurting someone with older umbrella designs.

Psychology: Everyday rules often become mystical when they are repeated without explanation and attached to a warning of bad luck.

Why It Persists: It remains vivid because it turns an ordinary object into something briefly dramatic and forbidden.

The Evil Eye

History: Across many cultures, envy and attention were believed to carry harmful force, especially toward beauty, success, or vulnerability.

CURATOR'S WARNING

Why Humans Still Believe

Superstitions are often dismissed as irrational, but they reveal something deeper about human behavior. In moments of uncertainty, people naturally look for patterns, causes, and rituals that make the world feel more predictable.

These beliefs persist not because they are scientifically proven, but because they are emotionally persuasive. They can reduce anxiety, create a sense of control, and connect people to family traditions, folklore, and cultural memory.

To study superstition is to study the human mind itself: its fears, its habits, its stories, and its tendency to give meaning to randomness.

This also connects to ideas such as pattern recognition bias, confirmation bias, and the tendency to remember events that seem to confirm what we already fear.

CURATOR'S JOURNAL

Process and Interpretation

This digital museum was designed as a focused exhibition using a Victorian fairy painting aesthetic: jewel tones, dark contrast, ornate framing, and an atmosphere of mystery. The goal was to create an experience that feels immersive rather than purely informational.

Each artifact is presented like a museum object. The front of each card introduces the superstition visually, while the reverse reveals deeper interpretation through historical and psychological context. This structure invites viewers to move from appearance to meaning, much like reading a museum label after first seeing an object.

The exhibit uses curation as a design strategy, treating superstition not as random folklore but as evidence of how humans respond to fear, uncertainty, and the need for control. The result is meant to feel like an archive of beliefs: eerie, beautiful, and revealing.
