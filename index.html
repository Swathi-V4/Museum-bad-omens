CURATOR'S WARNING

Why Humans Still Believe

Superstitions are often dismissed as irrational, but they reveal something deeper about human behavior. In moments of uncertainty, people naturally look for patterns, causes, and rituals that make the world feel more predictable.

These beliefs persist not because they are scientifically proven, but because they are emotionally persuasive. They can reduce anxiety, create a sense of control, and connect people to family traditions, folklore, and cultural memory.

To study superstition is to study the human mind itself: its fears, its habits, its stories, and its tendency to give meaning to randomness.

This also connects to ideas such as pattern recognition bias, confirmation bias, and the tendency to remember events that seem to confirm what we already fear.

CURATOR'S JOURNAL

Process and Interpretation

This digital museum was designed as a focused exhibition using a Victorian fairy painting aesthetic: jewel tones, dark contrast, ornate framing, and an atmosphere of mystery.

Each artifact is presented like a museum object. The front introduces the superstition visually, while the reverse reveals historical and psychological context.

The exhibit treats superstition not as random folklore but as evidence of how humans respond to fear, uncertainty, and the need for control.
