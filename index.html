Cabinet of Omens

A Digital Museum of Bad Luck Superstitions

Across cultures and centuries, humans have created superstitions to make sense of fear, risk, and uncertainty. This digital museum explores how symbols of bad luck reveal deeper psychological patterns in human behavior.

The Number 13

History: The fear of 13, known as triskaidekaphobia, has roots in Norse mythology and Christianity, where 13 guests at a table symbolized betrayal and chaos.

Psychology: This superstition reflects pattern-seeking behavior and the human tendency to associate coincidence with causation.

Black Cats

History: In medieval Europe, black cats were associated with witches and evil spirits, leading to their reputation as omens of bad luck.

Psychology: Fear of black cats demonstrates how cultural narratives can shape perception and reinforce irrational beliefs.

Cutting Nails at Night

History: In some Asian cultures, cutting nails at night was believed to invite spirits or misfortune, possibly due to poor lighting in earlier times.

Psychology: This reflects how practical concerns (like safety in darkness) evolve into symbolic beliefs over time.

Breaking a Mirror

History: Ancient Romans believed mirrors held part of the soul, so breaking one would damage the spirit and bring seven years of bad luck.

Psychology: This superstition reflects the human tendency to assign meaning to personal identity and symbolic objects.
