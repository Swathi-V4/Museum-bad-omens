WHY THIS MATTERS

The Psychology of Superstition

Superstitions are often dismissed as irrational, but they reveal something deeper about human behavior. In moments of uncertainty, people naturally look for patterns, causes, and rituals that make the world feel more predictable.

These beliefs persist not because they are scientifically proven, but because they are emotionally persuasive. They can reduce anxiety, create a sense of control, and connect people to family traditions, folklore, and cultural memory.

Studying superstition means studying how fear, storytelling, repetition, and social influence shape the way humans interpret the unknown.

This also connects to psychological ideas such as pattern recognition bias, confirmation bias, and the human tendency to impose meaning on randomness.

COLLECTION OF MISFORTUNE

Gallery of Superstitions

The Number 13

The Number 13

History: Fear of 13, known as triskaidekaphobia, is often linked to Western religious and mythological traditions in which the number signals disorder, imbalance, or betrayal.

Psychology: This reflects pattern-seeking behavior and the tendency to remember negative events more strongly when they confirm an existing fear.

Why It Persists: The number is still avoided in buildings, hotels, and travel settings, which helps the belief feel culturally reinforced and real.

Black Cats

Black Cats

History: In parts of medieval Europe, black cats became associated with witchcraft, night, and dark omens, turning an ordinary animal into a symbol of danger.

Psychology: This shows how social storytelling can attach fear to visual symbols and create strong emotional associations that outlast their original context.

Why It Persists: Because black cats are visually striking and repeatedly used in folklore and media, the superstition stays recognizable across generations.

Cutting Nails at Night

Cutting Nails at Night

History: In some Asian cultures, cutting nails after dark was believed to invite bad luck or danger, possibly because poor lighting once made the act risky or impractical.

Psychology: Practical warnings can slowly become symbolic beliefs when they are repeated through family instruction and tied to imagined consequences.

Why It Persists: The superstition survives because it blends daily routine with tradition, making it easy to pass down as both advice and cautionary belief.

Breaking a Mirror

Breaking a Mirror

History: Ancient cultures often viewed mirrors as spiritually significant objects connected to identity and the soul, so breaking one was thought to damage more than glass.

Psychology: Humans project emotional meaning onto personal objects, especially ones tied to reflection and self-image, which makes the superstition feel intimate and unsettling.

Why It Persists: A shattered mirror is dramatic, memorable, and visually symbolic, making the story easy to repeat and hard to forget.

CURATORIAL NOTES

Process and Interpretation

This digital museum was designed as a focused exhibition using a Victorian fairy painting aesthetic: jewel tones, dark contrast, ornate framing, and an atmosphere of mystery. The goal was to create an experience that feels immersive rather than purely informational.

Each artifact is presented like a museum object. The front of each card introduces the superstition visually, while the reverse reveals deeper interpretation through historical and psychological context. This structure invites viewers to move from appearance to meaning.

The exhibit uses curation as a design strategy, treating superstition not as random folklore but as evidence of how humans respond to fear, uncertainty, and the need for control.
